Lesson 4: Real-Life Role Plays:Reclaiming Space in Masculine Rooms

Why we practice:
You don’t rise to the occasion—you fall to the level of your preparation. So we rehearse—not to sound robotic—but to rewire the nervous system. Because when we’re under pressure (especially in male-dominated rooms), our body will default to old patterns:

  • Shrinking
  • Apologizing
  • People-pleasing
  • Over-explaining
  • Even if our mind knows better.

This section is your safe space to practice powerful presence, so when you’re in the real moment—you’re already embodied, prepared, and anchored in your worth. You can do these exercises with:

You can do these exercises with:

  • A trusted friend or peer
  • A coach or mentor
  • Or even solo—in the mirror, on voice notes, or in front of a camera

Role Play 1: Pitching Your Idea in a Male-Dominated Meeting

Scenario:

You're the only woman (or one of a few) in a strategy meeting. You have an idea that supports team goals, and you’re ready to share it—clearly and confidently.

Your Aligned, Assertive Script:

“Here’s what I’m proposing based on the data we’ve gathered and the feedback we’ve received. It aligns with the department’s objectives and timeline, and I believe it’s a strong next step.”

Embodiment Tips:

  • Speak on an exhale—it grounds your tone.
  • Keep your voice steady. Don’t rush.
  • Pause after you speak. Let the silence work in your favor.

This pause communicates confidence. It says, “I’ve said what needs to be said.”

Now… imagine a male colleague repeats your idea, and others credit him.

Instead of shrinking, try this gracefully assertive reframe:

“Thanks for reinforcing that point, Tom—I actually mentioned that earlier, and I’m glad it’s resonating. Let’s move forward with it.”

That’s not confrontation. That’s correction—with calm, grounded clarity.

Role Play 2: Asking for a Raise or Promotion

Scenario:

You’ve taken on more responsibilities. Your results speak for themselves. Now, it’s time to initiate the compensation or promotion conversation.

Your Empowered Script:

“Over the last six months, I’ve taken on new leadership responsibilities and have consistently delivered results in X, Y, and Z. I’d like to initiate a conversation around compensation to reflect the value I’m bringing to the team.”

What You Don’t Say:

  • “I was just wondering if maybe…”
  • “I hope it’s okay to ask…”
  • “I don’t mean to be pushy, but…”

These soften your authority. You don’t need to make yourself small to be heard.

Instead, speak from your center:

Breathe deeply. Sit tall. Hold steady eye contact. Keep your tone warm, but firm. You are not asking for a favor. You are naming your worth.

Bonus Tip:

Place a hand on your belly before walking in. Feel your breath. Ground your hips. This brings you out of your head (where doubt lives) and into your body (where wisdom lives).

Role Play 3: Handling Being Talked Over or Dismissed

Scenario:

You begin making a point, and someone (often male) cuts in and takes over.

Empowered Comeback:

“I’d like to finish my point—then I’m happy to hear your thoughts.”

OR

“Let me complete my thought and then we can circle back to that.”

Why this works:

It’s not aggressive. It’s firm. It holds your place in the room without needing to dominate the room.

Optional Script: Reclaiming Authority with a Soft Spine + Strong Voice

“I’d like to circle back to what I shared earlier. It feels like it might have been missed, and I want to make sure we’re aligned before we move forward.”

Use this when:

  • You’re dismissed mid-conversation
  • Someone bypasses your idea
  • You’re not being credited for your contribution

This script doesn’t attack—it re-centers you as a voice of authority.

How to Practice These Role Plays:

1. Choose one scenario per day.

2. Speak it out loud—in your full voice.

3. Record yourself if possible. Play it back.

Notice: Is your tone rushed? Quiet? Over-polite?

Then try again, slower, deeper, more grounded.

4. Practice saying it while:

  • Walking (to simulate movement and breath)
  • In front of a mirror (to observe posture and facial expressions)
  • Sitting at your desk (to simulate a real Zoom call or office setting)

The more you rehearse, the more embodied it becomes. And embodiment is what shifts performance into power.

Reflection:

This isn’t about becoming someone else. It’s about becoming more of you in every room, in every role, with every word. When you reclaim your voice, you don’t just advocate for yourself—you inspire every woman watching you to do the same. You are not too much. You are not asking for too much. You are becoming the woman your ancestors prayed for—and the one your future self is proud of.
