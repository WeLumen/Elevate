Lesson 5: Change management to address digital selling

In this session, we’ll be exploring a critical success factor for any organization transitioning into digital commerce: Change Management.

Often misunderstood as a corporate buzzword, change management is, in reality, a cornerstone of effective leadership—particularly in the context of launching new digital initiatives. Whether we’re integrating a new sales channel, introducing innovative products, or realigning our commercial strategy, one truth remains constant: organizations don’t change—people do. And for that change to occur, we need to manage it strategically.

This is especially true in small and medium-sized enterprises, where team dynamics are closely knit, and roles are often deeply ingrained. Humans, by nature, are resistant to change. Which means, when initiating a digital sales transformation, leaders must intentionally bring the organization along with them. This requires motivation, alignment, and a willingness to step outside the comfort zone—at every level of the business.

The Three Pillars of Driving Change in E-Commerce

To successfully embed digital sales into your commercial framework, you must activate change in three key areas:

1. Create an Environment That Supports Change

The foundation of any transformation is urgency. Your teams must understand that the transition to digital commerce is not optional—it is critical for survival and growth. There are several compelling arguments to drive this sense of urgency:

  • Customer behavior has shifted. Convenience and immediacy dominate the purchasing landscape—if we don’t meet our customers online, we risk losing them.
  • The post-pandemic economy accelerated digital adoption. Organizations that fail to adapt risk falling behind.
  • Digital commerce isn’t a trend—it’s the future. In many industries, it's already the present.
  • Enhancing our digital presence is a clear demonstration of our commitment to innovation and customer-centricity.

These are universal principles—whether you're running a boutique, a manufacturing company, or a food service operation. However, to make the case internally, these points must be tailored with market-specific data that resonate with your team’s context.

Communicating this consistently—not in a single meeting, but as a sustained initiative—is essential. Form a core team of internal influencers to amplify the message, and allow this shared vision to become part of the company’s daily conversations. Build and share a clear roadmap: what digital transformation means for your business and how it will benefit customers, teams, and the company at large.

2. Engage and Align the Team

True transformation doesn’t happen through systems—it happens through people. Everyone in the organization must be engaged from day one. A critical mistake is selective communication—informing certain departments or individuals first, which can foster a sense of exclusion or hierarchy. Instead, roll out the project vision simultaneously across all levels, emphasizing that every team member plays a vital role in its success.

Transparency is key. Avoid back-channel updates through informal platforms like WhatsApp, and instead cultivate a formal, inclusive communication plan. When people feel seen, heard, and valued, their commitment to the project increases significantly.

3. Activate and Sustain the Change

Once the groundwork is laid and the team is aligned, it's time to operationalize the vision. This requires not just delegation, but empowerment. Encourage each department to take ownership of its role in the digital sales process. Co-create solutions. Invite feedback. When employees feel that the project belongs to them, their engagement and accountability increase exponentially.

Support this engagement with resources—tools, training, and authority to remove obstacles. Track and celebrate small wins along the way. Weekly progress updates, however minor, help maintain momentum and demonstrate that the vision is becoming reality. These micro-achievements are essential for long-term motivation and visibility.

And finally, ensure that this change is not a one-off event but a sustainable evolution. Assign roles based on digital affinity and commercial acumen, but foster collaboration between tech-savvy and traditionally operational team members. By building a culture of cross-functional learning, you ensure that your digital channel becomes an integrated, lasting asset within your commercial strategy.

Make the Change Endure

To secure the future of your digital sales initiative, you must tie its outcomes to business performance. Regularly highlight how the new digital channel is contributing to increased sales, production efficiency, or customer satisfaction. Make it tangible. Make it relevant. Make it matter.

Because when people can clearly see the impact of their contributions—and how digital transformation is strengthening the company they care about—they will continue to support, innovate, and drive it forward.
