Lesson 4: Addressing the digital sales dilemma: proprietary website or marketplace?

As you're well aware, digital commerce presents immense opportunities for both B2B and B2C companies. Its ability to extend market reach, increase sales efficiency, and optimize customer engagement makes it a strategic imperative for modern businesses.

However, before diving into the digital sales landscape, every business must navigate a crucial strategic question: Should we launch our own e-commerce website or start by leveraging an existing marketplace?

This decision isn't one-size-fits-all. It depends on your company's goals, resources, stage of digital maturity, and long-term vision. Today, we’ll explore the key factors to consider so that you can make the most informed and strategic choice for your business.

Marketplace vs. Own E-commerce Platform: The Strategic Trade-Off

If your business is already taking its first steps toward digitalization, you’re likely evaluating whether to invest in building your own online store or to enter the digital market through a third-party marketplace.

Marketplaces such as Amazon, Mercado Libre, eBay, Flipkart, Alibaba, and Walmart provide immediate access to vast customer bases. You can open a seller account and begin listing products with relative ease and speed. This is a clear advantage in terms of time-to-market and lower upfront investment.

In contrast, launching your own e-commerce site means full control over branding, user experience, and customer data—but it also requires you to manage infrastructure, marketing, logistics, and ongoing operations. Tools like Shopify, Wix, and BigCommerce have made it significantly easier to set up and manage your own store without advanced programming skills. These platforms also integrate digital marketing functionalities to increase your visibility and customer engagement.

That said, building and maintaining a proprietary platform involves initial capital expenditure, recurring maintenance costs, and a greater need for internal management capabilities.

Key Advantages of Selling via Marketplaces

Let’s first examine the primary advantages of using established marketplaces:

  • Low Entry Barriers: Setting up a seller profile is fast, cost-effective, and doesn’t require complex development work.
  • Built-In Traffic & Trust: Marketplaces attract millions of daily visitors. Their established brand trust can transfer to your products—especially helpful when you're new to digital sales.
  • Reduced Marketing Overhead: Sellers can benefit from the platform’s existing ecosystem without immediately investing in their own advertising and communication strategies.
  • Operational Support: Marketplaces offer ready-to-use tools such as catalog management, secure payment systems, and integrated logistics. These features streamline your operations from day one.
  • Cybersecurity: High security standards protect both your transactions and your reputation. However, whether you choose a marketplace or a standalone site, cybersecurity must be a top priority. A single data breach can devastate your business. In fact, ransom demands in cyberattacks targeting small businesses often start at €10,000, with costs rising rapidly for larger companies.

Marketplace Disadvantages to Consider

Despite their appeal, marketplaces come with notable limitations:

  • Intense Competition: You’re placed directly alongside global competitors, including manufacturers selling at cost or below. The visibility of competitors and pricing pressures can be overwhelming, making it difficult to stand out without sacrificing margins.
  • Unpredictable Sales & Conversions: Traffic volume does not guarantee consistent conversion rates. Fluctuations in visibility and algorithm changes mean your sales can vary unpredictably.
  • Additional Fees: Many marketplaces charge listing fees, commission on sales, and even setup fees before you close a single transaction.
  • Limited Brand Control: Marketplaces prioritize product and price over brand identity. Customization of your seller profile is minimal, and promotional visibility is often dominated by sponsored listings—sometimes even from competitors replicating your product features.
  • Restricted Access to Customer Data: This is one of the biggest trade-offs. You rarely gain access to customer information, making it difficult to develop loyalty, remarketing strategies, or direct communications. The marketplace owns the relationship—not you.
  • Loyalty to the Platform, Not the Seller: Marketplaces cultivate customer loyalty to their ecosystem. Even if you make a sale, the buyer is more likely to remember the marketplace—not your brand.

Owning Your Platform: Benefits and Considerations

Now let’s assess the benefits of building your own e-commerce site:

  • Full Brand Ownership: You control every aspect of the customer journey—from the first click to post-purchase follow-up.
  • Customer Relationship Development: You own the data, enabling CRM, loyalty programs, personalized marketing, and retargeting strategies.
  • Greater Profit Margins: No commission fees, fewer limitations on pricing strategy, and better long-term ROI potential.
  • Agility and Customization: You can tailor your site’s UX/UI, messaging, product presentation, and even A/B test conversion strategies in real time.

However, all of this comes at the cost of higher operational responsibilities and initial financial investment.

Final Thoughts: Choosing the Right Path

There is no universally right answer. The best choice depends on your strategic goals, operational capabilities, and digital maturity. In some cases, a hybrid approach may be ideal—using marketplaces for immediate exposure while gradually building your own platform to nurture long-term brand equity.

What’s important is making a data-informed, forward-thinking decision that aligns with your company's growth strategy and customer relationship priorities.

If your business is ready to build sustainable digital capabilities, the long-term value of owning your e-commerce ecosystem cannot be overstated.

Now that we’ve reviewed the advantages and limitations of marketplaces, it’s time to evaluate the strategic implications of developing your own e-commerce platform. This decision is not merely technical—it's a critical commercial choice that can significantly shape your brand positioning, customer relationships, and long-term profitability.

First and foremost, owning your e-commerce website grants you full control over the entire digital sales funnel. From the first point of contact to post-purchase service, every touchpoint can be tailored to reflect your brand's identity, tone, and values. This control allows for the design of personalized customer journeys, which can substantially enhance engagement and conversion rates.

Second, customization becomes a true competitive differentiator. Unlike the limitations imposed by marketplace templates, your own store can be fully adapted to reflect your visual identity, value proposition, and unique selling points. The extent to which you stand out is no longer constrained by platform rules, but only by your strategic creativity.

Third, functionality is in your hands. Most modern e-commerce platforms (such as Shopify, WooCommerce, Wix, or BigCommerce) offer plug-and-play extensibility through modules and apps. If your business requires more complex or bespoke functionalities, you can engage a specialized development agency to create custom modules tailored to your operational needs—something marketplaces simply cannot offer.

Fourth, and perhaps most strategically important, is data ownership. Direct access to customer data allows for in-depth analytics, segmentation, and personalized communication. This opens the door to advanced digital marketing strategies, precise remarketing, behavior-based email campaigns, and loyalty programs—each enhancing customer lifetime value and deepening brand loyalty.

Challenges of Owning an E-commerce Platform

  • The first is investment. Building and maintaining an independent online store requires upfront capital and ongoing operational expenses. While SaaS solutions reduce entry barriers, they still demand resources—time, budget, and technical oversight. You'll need to account for hosting, domain registration, payment gateway fees, SSL certification, and regular maintenance.
  • The second is visibility. Simply launching a website doesn’t guarantee traffic. Unlike marketplaces that come with built-in audiences, your site will require a robust digital marketing strategy to drive awareness and conversions. This includes SEO, content creation, paid media, email marketing, and social media engagement—each with its own cost and learning curve. For brands without strong market recognition or a differentiated product, this can be a significant hurdle.
  • Third, there’s the matter of customer service. With full control comes full responsibility. You must provide efficient support for inquiries, complaints, refunds, and technical issues. While outsourcing is an option, the responsibility for service quality ultimately falls on your team, and subpar support can quickly erode trust and loyalty.

In conclusion, both options—marketplace integration and owning an e-commerce website—offer distinct strategic benefits and challenges. The decision should align with your business model, brand goals, financial capacity, and customer acquisition strategy.

For many companies, particularly SMEs, a hybrid approach proves to be the most pragmatic: leveraging the reach and low entry cost of marketplaces while simultaneously building an owned channel for long-term brand development and customer retention.

As commercial leaders, our role is to make informed, data-backed decisions. The digital commerce landscape rewards those who balance agility with foresight.
