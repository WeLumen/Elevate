Lesson 5: Day 5

Step 5 in the journey of healing from interstitial cystitis and chronic pelvic pain. Today’s focus is on a core piece of the puzzle: the sympathetic nervous system and its connection to pain.

Let’s begin with a playful metaphor to illustrate what happens in our bodies:

An eagle, a coyote, and a possum walk into a bar...

These animals represent the three primary responses of the nervous system in the face of perceived danger:

  • Eagle – the fight response
  • Coyote – the flight response
  • Possum – the freeze response

These instinctive reactions once helped us survive physical threats. But in modern life, many of our “threats” are emotional, social, or psychological—and our bodies still react the same way.

How the Stress Response Affects the Body

When the nervous system perceives danger, even if the threat isn’t physical, it triggers physiological changes:

  • Increased heart rate
  • Blood redirected to the legs (preparing to run)
  • Reduced digestion
  • Cortisol and adrenaline release
  • Sweating, dry mouth, nausea
  • Jaw tension, ringing ears, fatigue, or hives

These are all signals from the body that the nervous system is stuck in fight, flight, or freeze. For those dealing with chronic pain, these symptoms may feel familiar—especially during flare-ups. This heightened state can contribute to persistent pain and tension.

Creating Safety in the Body

To begin healing, the nervous system must shift from a state of threat to a state of safety. The body must learn that it is no longer in danger. This transition is key to calming chronic pain and reducing stress-related symptoms.

Here are simple, effective tools to help create a sense of internal safety:

1. Intentional Breathing

Place a hand over your heart or chest to create a sense of connection. Breathe deeply:

  • Inhale for a count of 4
  • Exhale for a count of 6 or 7

Repeat slowly, saying affirmations like:

  • “I am safe.”
  • “I am supported.”
  • “I’ve overcome challenges before, and I will again.”

2. Visualization

Start your day with a mental picture of calm. Imagine yourself moving through the day peacefully, handling challenges with confidence and ease. This helps rewire the brain to expect safety instead of threat.

3. Gentle Movement

Simple practices such as:

  • Yoga
  • Meditation
  • Nature walks

These support nervous system regulation and bring awareness back to the body in a soothing way.

4. Comfort and Self-Care

Small acts of self-kindness—curling up with a cozy blanket, taking a warm bath, or enjoying soft textures—send signals of safety to the brain. These can calm both physical and emotional discomfort.

Reflection and Journaling Prompts

To integrate this step into your journey, consider journaling around these prompts:

  • Where do you physically carry stress in your body?
  • What sensations arise during times of stress or anxiety?
  • Are there memories, beliefs, or emotions that seem connected to these sensations?
  • What does safety feel like in your body? How can you invite more of it in?

Writing about these experiences can uncover deeper patterns and bring clarity to your healing path.

You don’t have to get this perfect. Healing is not a linear path—it’s a process of learning, unlearning, and gently showing up for yourself. Every breath, every pause, and every act of self-kindness helps guide your nervous system out of defense and back into peace.

You are safe. You are resilient. And your body is listening.
