Lesson 1: Social Comparison Theory: The Mirror We Hold Up to Ourselves

From a psychological and evolutionary standpoint, competition is not only natural—it's human. But how it shows up in our lives is often deeply shaped by both our biology and the cultural narratives we've inherited. As women, we don’t just compete differently—we’ve been taught to. Understanding why we sometimes find ourselves caught in comparison, rivalry, or even silent judgment of other women is the first step to reclaiming our power and rewriting the script.

Social Comparison Theory: The Mirror We Hold Up to Ourselves

Psychologist Leon Festinger’s Social Comparison Theory tells us that humans have a fundamental drive to evaluate themselves, often by comparing to others. But here’s where gender socialization kicks in: women are conditioned—especially in media-saturated, appearance-focused cultures—to constantly assess their value based on external validation. Beauty. Status. Relationship status. Motherhood. Success. And the comparisons we make? They're rarely upward in a constructive way. Often, they’re lateral—woman to woman—fueling unnecessary tension.

As a coach, I see this all the time: brilliant, capable women doubting themselves not because they’re unqualified, but because someone on Instagram “seems to have it all together.” But remember: comparison is a mirror, not a measure. When we feel triggered by another woman’s success or beauty, it’s often reflecting back our own unrealized desires or suppressed potential. That’s an opportunity—not a threat.

Relational Aggression: The Hidden Battlefield

Unlike the more overt forms of male-dominated competition (like physical aggression or hierarchical dominance), women are often socialized to compete covertly. Psychologists call this relational aggression—things like gossip, exclusion, passive-aggressiveness, or subtle undermining. Why? Because in many societies, assertive or dominant behavior in women is penalized. We’re called “bossy,” “too much,” or “intimidating.” So instead, many women unconsciously learn to express rivalry in quieter—but still harmful—ways.

But here's the truth: this behavior isn’t a flaw in our character. It’s an adaptation. An outdated survival mechanism. When you understand that, you can disarm it. We weren’t born to tear each other down—we were taught to compete for limited resources (love, attention, power) in a system that was never designed with our wholeness in mind.

Healing this means learning how to hold space for both our power and someone else’s. It means understanding that another woman shining doesn’t dim your light—it shows you what’s possible.

Internalized Misogyny: The Cultural Virus

One of the most insidious psychological forces we must confront is internalized misogyny—the subconscious absorption of sexist beliefs, values, and judgments that pit women against each other. When we grow up in a culture that devalues femininity, glorifies male-centered ambition, and normalizes female rivalry (think: "mean girls," "catfights," or "queen bees"), we internalize those messages. They become part of the inner voice that says:

  • “She thinks she’s better than me.”
  • “I don’t trust women like that.”
  • “I’m not like other girls.”

But these are not your thoughts—they're cultural programming. And once you become aware of them, you get to reprogram. You get to choose to stand with other women, not against them. To celebrate, not compete. To collaborate, not compare. That’s where true feminine power lies—in the unshakable belief that when one of us rises, we all rise.

From Survival to Sisterhood

The competitive behaviors women often display toward one another aren’t rooted in weakness—they’re rooted in survival. But we are no longer living in the same world our mothers or grandmothers did. We have the tools, the awareness, and the collective wisdom to evolve beyond these outdated dynamics.

As a woman—and especially as a leader or coach—you are called to model a new paradigm of empowered sisterhood. One where competition is healthy, not harmful. Where support is stronger than sabotage. Where we stop seeing each other as rivals and start recognizing each other as allies.

Because the truth is: empowered women don’t fear each other’s power—they fuel it.
