Module 3: Sociological and Structural Factors: How Culture Shapes Women’s Relationships with Each Other

Lesson 1: Stereotypes and Media Narratives: The Myth of the Catfight

As women, the way we relate to one another doesn't exist in a vacuum. Our behaviors, expectations, and even insecurities are often shaped by broader cultural narratives and deeply embedded societal structures. If you've ever felt tension, competition, or mistrust in your relationships with other women—know this: it's not just personal, it's also systemic. Understanding these forces is the first step toward reclaiming our power and building authentic, supportive sisterhoods. Let’s unpack the key factors that shape how we show up with and for other women.

1. Stereotypes and Media Narratives: The Myth of the Catfight

From the time we're little girls, we're bombarded with images of female conflict: the "catfight," the manipulative "Queen Bee," the vindictive "mean girl." These aren’t just silly plotlines in teen dramas—they are cultural scripts that teach us how to behave. They tell us that other women are competition, that we can’t trust each other, and that there's always a hierarchy we must fight to climb.

These narratives are designed to keep us divided. Why? Because divided women are disempowered women. When we internalize these stereotypes, we don’t just distrust other women—we start to doubt ourselves. We waste energy navigating false rivalries instead of collaborating and rising together.

As an empowerment coach, I challenge you to pause and ask: Whose story am I living out when I feel threatened by another woman? Is this truly mine—or something I was taught to believe?

2. Tokenism in Leadership: When There's Only Room for One

In many workplaces, particularly in male-dominated industries, there's an unspoken rule: there’s only room for one woman at the top. Whether it's the boardroom, the stage, or the sports field, women are often treated like rare exceptions instead of the rule. That scarcity creates pressure. It sends the message that we must compete for the one seat at the table, rather than build a bigger table.

This is tokenism at work. When a system only allows a few women into positions of influence, those women may—understandably—feel threatened by others who might "replace" them. But the truth is, real leadership doesn’t hoard power; it multiplies it. It takes courage and inner work to shift from scarcity to abundance, to go from “There’s only room for me” to “Let’s rise together.”

We break this pattern by supporting each other’s ascent, not by protecting our turf. Women don’t need to be the only—we need to be many.

3. Workplace Bias: A System Rigged Against Women

Here’s something that’s hard but necessary to say: Studies consistently show that both men and women can be biased against women in positions of power. These unconscious biases shape hiring decisions, performance evaluations, and even how we perceive confidence and assertiveness.

Because women know—on some level—that the playing field is unequal, we often feel we must overperform, and sometimes that pressure gets redirected at other women. You might feel the need to be “better than her,” to prove your worth, to show you’re not like the others.

But this isn’t empowerment—it’s survival in a broken system. And the antidote isn’t self-silencing or outshining other women. It’s collective courage. It’s mentoring, uplifting, and sponsoring one another. It’s knowing that when one of us wins, we all expand what’s possible.

Rewriting the Story: From Competition to Collaboration

Empowered women aren’t immune to these forces—but we become conscious of them. We learn to spot the patterns, challenge the programming, and make new choices. We refuse to play small or pit ourselves against each other for a slice of a broken pie.

Sisterhood is not just a feel-good buzzword—it is a revolutionary act. When women support women, when we lead together, when we dismantle the myths that divide us, we create ripples of change that affect families, communities, and entire cultures.

If you’re ready to rewrite your narrative around female relationships, start with this truth: You are not my rival. You are my reflection. And together, we rise.
