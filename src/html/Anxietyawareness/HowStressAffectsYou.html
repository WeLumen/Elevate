How Stress Affects You

This lesson will explore the ways in which stress can impact various areas of an individual’s life. This can include the impact to physical and mental health, as well as relationships and working life.

This section will look into the internal and external demands faced by people in their daily lives, as well as the ways these can be linked to stress.

Physical Effects

It is a commonly known fact that people experiencing stress will be impacted both mentally and physically. Mental health struggles typically happen when the person is unable to deal with stressors.

While acute stress can go away on its own, many types of stress do not disappear, requiring advice and support to overcome its effects.

Stress may weaken the immune system or lead to serious issues like high blood pressure, heart attacks, or strokes. Many report feeling tired or run down without clear physical illnesses.

Mental Health Effects

Stress often appears alongside mental health conditions. Anxiety and depression are most commonly linked to stress. These often work in a cycle, worsening one another.

For example, a person may begin feeling stressed, which causes anxiety. Anxiety may lead to isolation and depression, which increases stress even more.

One in five people in the UK are likely to experience this cycle at some point in their lives.

Internal demands often relate to self-esteem and confidence. A person with realistic expectations is less likely to feel stressed than one who constantly aims for perfection or sets unrealistic goals.

For example, a single mother working full time may strive for promotions while expecting high academic success from her children. If neither goal is met, she may feel like a failure, leading to reduced confidence and added stress.

External Demands

Major life changes such as moving home, starting a new job, or losing a loved one can cause stress. When unexpected or overwhelming, these can lead to chronic stress and mental health issues.

Work and education are also significant stressors. Feeling trapped in a job, facing team conflicts, or dealing with overwhelming workloads can make people feel helpless.

Being Pessimistic

Pessimism increases the likelihood of stress. Pessimistic individuals focus on negatives and resist adapting, which leads to unnecessary worry and stress.

For example, a pessimist may cancel plans due to rain and feel their vacation is ruined. An optimist may adapt and find indoor activities, staying positive throughout.

Chronic worry from pessimism leads to stress by making individuals focus on worst-case scenarios and miss out on positive opportunities.

Effects of Stress on Our Actions and Lives

Stress changes behavior. People may become irritable, withdrawn, or emotional without clear reasons. These changes are most noticeable to close friends and family.

Stress can affect job performance, making it difficult to focus or remember things. This can lead to decreased work quality and absenteeism.

Stress also affects relationships. A person may withdraw from loved ones, causing misunderstandings and strained communication. This isolation worsens stress and can contribute to depression.

Negative Self-Talk

Negative self-talk involves putting oneself down and believing they are unworthy or incapable. This mindset leads to low confidence and high stress, preventing people from enjoying life or trying new things.

Major Players in Causing Stress

  • Money problems: Debt, bills, and social pressure to spend can create stress.
  • Family issues: Disagreements, caring responsibilities, and fatigue from parenting are common stressors.
  • Parenting challenges: Stress can cause irritability or overreaction toward children, creating more stress and conflict.

In this lesson we have looked at:

  • Mental and physical health – When a person is experiencing stress, there are many changes that take place, mentally and physically.
  • Behavioural changes – People often behave in new or unusual ways when going through stress. This can be particularly noticeable for close friends or family.
  • Work problems – Work problems are a common cause of stress, but work can also suffer as a result of stress.
  • Negative self-talk – Negative self-talk is when somebody tells themselves that they cannot cope with tasks and that they are worthless.
  • Pessimism – This is the ‘glass half empty’ outlook on life, focusing on negative aspects of events. Those who are pessimistic are a lot more likely to experience stress than an optimistic person.
