An Introduction to Stress

This lesson will outline what stress is, explaining the various types as well as the symptoms and effects it has. Stress is the effect of the adrenaline hormone being released into the body.

Stress can be positive if the adrenaline helps a person to feel more motivated or become more focused. Alternatively, stress can also negatively impact people’s lives, making the immune system weaker and stopping people sleeping, also known as insomnia.

We will explore the positive and negative effects of stress on individuals, outlining the various kinds of stress.

What is Stress?

Stress can impact people at any time in life and can be caused by many factors. Of the 12 million people who have appointments with a GP for mental health reasons, most of these people have stress-related issues. Experiencing stress can cause major difficulties in the way people lead their lives.

It can be challenging to correctly diagnose stress due to countless debates on what exactly stress is and how it can be noticed.

There are many definitions available to explain what stress is, including:

  • “Something that causes strong feelings of worry or anxiety.”
  • “A state of mental tension and worry caused by problems in an individual’s life or work.”
  • “A state of mental or emotional strain or tension resulting from adverse or demanding circumstances.”
  • “The feelings that occur when the potential demands of a situation do not match with an individual’s ability to cope with it.”

While these definitions do vary somewhat, they all follow a similar theme. In this course, we will use the final definition of stress listed when referring to stress.

Symptom Categories

  • Cognitive: Poor concentration, continuous worry and/or anxiety, bad judgement, forgetfulness, an inability to be optimistic
  • Emotional: Mood swings, being irritable, feeling agitated, regularly feeling overwhelmed, feeling down, worry and anxiety
  • Behavioural: Eating too much or too little, sleeping for too long or not long enough, ignoring responsibilities, smoking or drinking to feel relaxed, nervous habits
  • Physical: Aching body and pains, tiredness, regular illnesses, reduced libido, feeling sick, being dizzy, fast heartbeat and chest pain

Acute and Chronic Stress

  • Acute: A one-time stressful occurrence
  • Chronic: A continued or prolonged period of stressful events

Examples of chronic stressors

  • Overworking or ‘burning out’
  • Abuse or neglect
  • Being unhappy with body image or weight
  • Financial struggles
  • Poor social life and lack of interaction
  • Insomnia or trouble sleeping
  • Family problems
  • Poor health
  • Caring for others
  • Relationship troubles
  • Fears and phobias
  • Lack of free time on an ongoing basis

Examples of acute stressors

  • Losing a loved one
  • A divorce or break-up
  • Moving home
  • Exams and public speaking
  • Upcoming deadlines
  • Car crashes without long-term implications
  • Experiencing a traumatic event
  • Poor health or a resolved diagnosis

Physical Effects of Acute Stress

  • Sweating
  • Pupil dilation
  • Fast heart rate
  • Quick breathing
  • Shaking or trembling
  • Digestive system constriction

But does stress actually affect my overall health and my immune system?

The short answer is "yes": Stress can be complicated to understand due to the fact that many symptoms that arise as a result of stress, can also work as triggers to worsen it.

Blood Pressure Categories

  • Normal: Below 120 / Below 80
  • Prehypertension: 120–139 / 80–89
  • Stage 1 hypertension: 140–159 / 90–99
  • Stage 2 hypertension: 160+ / 100+

Associated Conditions

  • Fatigue and tiredness
  • Skin conditions like eczema or psoriasis
  • Depression and anxiety
  • Panic disorders
  • Digestion issues, including IBS and coeliac disease

But can stress be beneficial?

Yes, in controlled, short bursts. Stress can improve motivation, concentration, alertness and even memory. It’s part of the body’s fight or flight system and releases adrenaline, norepinephrine and cortisol.

In this lesson we have looked at:

  • Acute stress – The natural response to dangerous or frightening experiences, lasting only a short period of time.
  • Chronic stress – A prolonged form of stress that can begin as a result of specific situations.
  • The impact of stress on the immune system – When it is in large amounts, the negative effects of stress outweigh the positive ones by far.
  • Blood pressure – Those with stress are much more likely to experience high blood pressure, or hypertension.
  • Positive effects of stress – Can exist in moderate, controllable doses and strengthen alertness and the immune system.
