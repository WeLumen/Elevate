Lesson 6: Setting Boundaries & Reclaiming Power

"Boundaries are love letters to yourself. They say: I matter. My energy is sacred."

Boundaries are not walls. They are not punishments. They are clear, compassionate containers that allow love, time, and energy to flow in alignment with your deepest truth. When you set a boundary, you’re not pushing others away—you are drawing yourself closer to the sacred center of your being.

Boundaries say: “I’m worthy of protection. I’m worthy of peace. I’m allowed to choose what I allow into my life.”
This is reclamation work. This is how we come home to ourselves.

Embodied Boundaries: The Practice of Saying No

Start in the mirror. Literally. Stand tall. Shoulders relaxed. Feet grounded. Look yourself in the eyes and say it out loud:

  • “No.”
  • “That’s not okay with me.”
  • “I’m choosing not to engage.”
  • “It’s okay if others are disappointed. I will not abandon myself.”

Feel the resonance of these words. Notice where in your body you contract or expand. This is nervous system work. Practice not just speaking the boundary—but inhabiting it. Let the “no” live in your spine. Let your heart stay open while your energy remains sovereign.

Scripts for Protection: Language that Liberates

Boundaries are often forged in small, everyday choices. It can help to have words ready—especially when you’re breaking patterns of people-pleasing or codependency.

Here are some powerful, clear boundary phrases you can use:

  • “I’m not available for this conversation.”
  • “That doesn’t work for me.”
  • “I need time to think.”

Repeat these until they feel like muscle memory. Say them in your car, your shower, your journal. Let your voice learn how to protect you.

Fawning Recovery: Healing the Reflex to Over-Accommodate

Many of us learned to survive by being agreeable. By softening our needs. By prioritizing peace over authenticity. This is what we call fawning—a trauma response that trades personal truth for perceived safety.

When you feel the urge to explain, fix, or overextend—pause. Ask yourself:

  • “What am I afraid will happen if I say no?”
  • “Whose disappointment am I trying to avoid?”
  • “Where in my body do I feel the fear?”

Often, this fear is ancestral. It's cellular. It’s your nervous system bracing for a punishment from your past. You are not in danger. You are allowed to say no—and survive.

Gentleness is essential here. You are not failing. You are rewiring—slowly, kindly, intentionally.

Sacred Space: You Are Allowed to Be Unavailable

Your time is not a public resource. Your energy is not a vending machine. Your heart is not a hallway with open doors.

Begin the sacred process of releasing what drains you. You can say: “I’m no longer available for this.”

  • Turning down invitations that feel heavy, even if you “have nothing else planned.”
  • Not responding to texts right away—or at all.
  • Cancelling recurring commitments that no longer align.
  • Saying, “Thank you for thinking of me, but I can’t commit to that right now.”
  • Choosing solitude over socialization without guilt.

Boundaries aren’t selfish—they are sacred. They ensure your presence is full, honest, and free.

The Invitation: Let Boundaries Be a Devotion

Think of boundaries as a devotional act—a practice of coming home to your center again and again. They are not a one-time fix but a daily choosing. A muscle you strengthen. A compass you follow.

You might feel guilt. You might feel fear. You might lose people who were only connected to you through your self-betrayal. Let it all move through you.

You are not here to be palatable. You are here to be whole.
You are worthy of peace, freedom, and alignment—and every time you say no to what drains you, you say yes to the life that honors your truth.
